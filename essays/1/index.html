Why Science Gets Stuck
Modern research may be optimizing the known
while neglecting the possible

From a $12 million missile to DeepSeek to the neuroscience of the daydream, the central bottleneck in science may not be intelligence, effort, or funding. It may be the way we search.

I was doing nothing.

Not reading a paper. Not running an experiment. I was letting my mind drift across worm biology, swarm robotics, warfare, neuromodulation — when something clicked that months of structured work hadn't produced. A connection between fields I had no reason to juxtapose. An insight that arrived not from effort but from its absence.

That daydream became the spine of my research.

This bothered me. It should bother you too.

Because if the most important cognitive event in months of serious work happened during the one hour nobody was measuring — working alone, no lab, no grant, rented compute — then we have a structural problem. Not a personal one. A systemic one.

This essay proposes one simple possibility: the central bottleneck in science may not be intelligence, effort, or funding. It may be search. We have built institutions highly capable of exploiting known directions. We have built almost nothing for exploring unknown ones.

§

I.

The $12,000,000 Problem

In recent conflicts, Iranian drones costing approximately $5,000 each have required THAAD interceptor missiles costing approximately $12,000,000 per shot to neutralise.

Read that again. Five thousand versus twelve million dollars.

A 2400x cost asymmetry. The defender cannot win this game through optimisation. Better missiles cost more. Cheaper drones cost less. The gradient — the direction that looks like improvement — points confidently upward and leads straight into a trap. This is not a failure of engineering. It is a failure of search strategy.

The emerging solution is laser-based directed energy defence. Cost per intercept: less than $1. A 12,000,000× cost reduction — achieved not by improving the existing missile but by returning to first principles. Someone stopped climbing the missile hill long enough to ask a genuinely different question. They found a different mountain entirely.

This phenomenon has a precise name in mathematics and physics. When a system under pressure reorganises into a qualitatively different state — not better, but structurally different — it is called a phase transition. Water doesn't become "more liquid" as temperature drops. It becomes ice. The THAAD-to-laser shift is the same kind of event: same problem, completely different solution space. Physicists, mathematicians, and biologists have studied these reorganisations carefully.[1] They share a common signature — a period of apparent stagnation followed by sudden structural change, triggered not by more effort but by a shift in the underlying conditions.

This is the pattern. Incumbents optimise the known. Constrained outsiders search for something else. The outsider doesn't always win. But when the landscape is deceptive — when the most direct path leads away from the real solution — the searcher beats the optimiser every time.

This pattern now appears in science.

Phase transitions in physics are described by bifurcation theory. A simple example is the double-well potential:

V(x, c) = x⁴ + c·x² When c is positive: one minimum — one obvious solution. The missile.
When c crosses zero: bifurcation point — the landscape is about to reorganise.
When c is negative: two minima emerge. The second one is the laser.

The constraint — limited budget, physical law, export ban — acts as the control parameter c. It doesn't slow the search. It changes the shape of the solution space, revealing minima that were always mathematically present but invisible to anyone climbing the first hill.

The same mathematics describes phase transitions in magnetism, protein folding, and the emergence of collective behaviour in biological swarms. It is a universal signature of systems that reorganise under pressure.

II.

The Same Thing Just Happened in AI

In January 2025, DeepSeek published a model matching GPT-4 on most benchmarks. Training cost: $6 million. GPT-4's estimated cost: $80–100 million. GPU hours used: 2.78 million versus approximately 60 million — a 21× difference.[2]

The reason was not superior talent. It was a constraint. The United States government had banned export of NVIDIA's most powerful chips to China. DeepSeek couldn't buy the weapon. So they had to search differently.

What they found under that pressure: a way to run only a fraction of the model's parameters for each calculation instead of all of them, a lower-precision arithmetic method nobody had validated at this scale, and a new algorithm that hid communication delays behind computation. None of these existed as standard practice before the constraint forced the search.

The export ban was not an obstacle that slowed progress. It was a reorganising pressure that revealed a different solution space — one that better-resourced labs had no incentive to explore.

Same problem. Different mountain.

This pattern recurs across domains and across history.

The Aravind Eye Care System in India couldn't afford Western hospital models — so they invented assembly-line cataract surgery at 1/10th the cost with equivalent outcomes, performing over 300,000 surgeries annually. M-Pesa in Kenya had no banking infrastructure — so they built mobile money from scratch, now used by 53 million people globally. Yuan Longping developed hybrid rice in China under severe resource constraints — feeding hundreds of millions without increasing inputs.

The common structure: constraint closes the obvious path, forcing search into territory that abundance never visits. The solution found there is often not just cheaper — it is structurally superior to what incumbents were optimising.

III.

Science Is Climbing the Wrong Hill

Modern science has more researchers, more data, more instruments, and more computational power than any previous generation. By many measures it is thriving.

Yet the same pattern — incumbents optimising the known while neglecting the possible — may now be operating inside science itself.

Any adaptive system must balance two tasks. Exploitation: refining what already works, scaling proven tools, extending known theories. Exploration: searching uncertain territory, combining distant ideas, following anomalies into unfamiliar terrain. A system that only exploits becomes efficient at climbing the nearest hill while missing taller mountains elsewhere. Science requires both.

Bloom, Jones, Van Reenen, and Webb (2020) put a number on this.[3] Research productivity — the number of researchers required to produce a given rate of improvement — has doubled approximately every 13 years since the 1930s across multiple fields. Moore's Law required 18 researchers in 1971 to sustain its pace. By 2014, 18 times more were needed. Michael Nielsen and Patrick Collison have argued independently that science may be delivering less per unit input than in earlier eras. Recent analyses of papers and patents suggest they are becoming less disruptive over time — more work building incrementally on existing trajectories rather than redirecting them. This is precisely what an over-exploitative system would produce.

By 2020: ~18× more researchers needed to sustain the same rate of progress as 1930.

The problem is partly one of metrics. Modern science is rich in measures: papers published, citations accumulated, grants won, benchmarks improved. These are useful — but they are not identical to discovery. Publication count is not insight. Benchmark gains are not always conceptual progress. When proxies become targets, behaviour adapts around them, often unconsciously. Studies of peer review and grantmaking have repeatedly found caution around unusually novel or interdisciplinary work — not from bad faith, but because genuinely uncertain ideas are genuinely difficult to evaluate in advance.

Here is how this plays out structurally. A young researcher choosing between two projects faces an asymmetry that has nothing to do with scientific merit. The first extends established work — clear methods, familiar reviewers, predictable publications, low reputational risk. The second crosses disciplines, depends on uncertain tools, and may fail entirely. Even in institutions that celebrate innovation rhetorically, the first project is often the prudent choice. Across thousands of such decisions, caution compounds. No institution needs to suppress originality deliberately. Incentives do the work quietly.

The failure modes are specific. I have experienced them working on brain-inspired AI. I have spent approximately 250 hours repeating experiments that others had almost certainly already abandoned — silently, invisibly, because there is no infrastructure for sharing structured failure. Negative results go unpublished and quietly repeated. Interdisciplinary work falls between funding categories. The most interesting questions — the ones that don't fit existing frameworks — get systematically deferred. An overcrowded hill absorbs enormous effort while nearby valleys remain unexplored.

This is not a funding problem. It is a search problem.

In reinforcement learning and decision theory, this tension has a formal name: the exploration-exploitation trade-off. The Gittins index theorem (Gittins, 1979) proves that the optimal policy for choosing between known and unknown options requires a systematic exploration bonus — a reward for trying what hasn't been tried, proportional to uncertainty.[4] Pure exploitation of the best-known option is mathematically guaranteed to underperform over time.

Science funding behaves like a system with the exploration bonus removed. Grants reward proximity to known objectives. Reviews reward legibility. Careers reward measurable output. The mathematics says this will converge to a local maximum.

Wuchty, Jones, and Uzzi (2007) analysed millions of scientific papers and found that while team science has grown dominant, solo and small-team work continues to produce disproportionately disruptive findings — suggesting the search function in science is being concentrated in structures less suited to exploratory discovery.[5]

IV.

The Brain Already Solved This

The brain is the only known system that has solved continuous learning, generalisation, and adaptation simultaneously — on 20 watts, across a human lifetime.

It does this through two architecturally separated modes.

The first is focused exploitation — precise, targeted, fast. This is the system active when you are running a planned experiment, writing a paper, optimising a known method. It climbs hills efficiently.

The second is something stranger. Neuroscientists call it the Default Mode Network. It becomes more active when you are doing nothing — resting, walking, letting your mind wander. It consumes approximately 20% of total brain energy at rest.[6] The brain pays a significant metabolic price to keep this system running when you appear to be idle.

Why? Because it performs a function the focused system cannot. It integrates across distant, apparently unrelated memory spaces. It is where unexpected connections form — between fields that have no obvious relationship, between a problem and a solution that doesn't look like one yet.

Jung-Beeman et al. (2004) captured the moment of insight in real time.[7] The "aha moment" is preceded by the brain actively suppressing its focused attention system — quieting exploitation so exploration can work.

A chemical switching system — involving dopamine, acetylcholine, and related signals — coordinates the two modes based on what the moment requires. Exploit when the path is clear. Explore when it isn't.

This architecture is not a metaphor for good research practice. It is the actual mechanism that biological intelligence runs on. Five hundred million years of evolution decided it was worth the metabolic cost.

Modern research institutions are designed almost entirely around the first mode. We have formalised execution. We have barely formalised discovery.

The Default Mode Network was initially dismissed as neural noise — an artifact of subtracting task-related activity from brain scans. It took years to recognise it as a distinct, functionally important system. Raichle et al. (2001) established its existence and baseline metabolic significance.[6] Subsequent research linked it to creative cognition, future simulation, social reasoning, and autobiographical memory consolidation.

The practical implication is uncomfortable: the cognitive state most associated with insight and novel connection-making is precisely the state that modern institutional design treats as unproductive. Protected thinking time, unstructured conversation, and cross-disciplinary wandering are not inefficiencies to be eliminated. They are the substrate of discovery.

A civilisation should not outsource its exploratory cognition to weekends and chance walks.

V.

The Algorithm Science Is Missing

Kenneth Stanley and Joel Lehman spent years building controllers for robots using evolutionary algorithms.[8,9] They kept hitting the same wall: the more precisely they defined the goal, the worse the search performed. The algorithm climbed confidently — and got permanently stuck.

So they tried something that felt almost irresponsible. They removed the objective entirely.

Instead of rewarding progress toward a goal, they rewarded only one thing: being different from everything explored before. They called it novelty search. Each candidate solution is scored not by how close it is to the target but by how far it is from an ever-growing archive of everything the search has already visited. The most novel candidate wins. Always.

The result was counterintuitive and reproducible. On deceptive landscapes — where the direct path toward the goal leads away from it — ignoring the objective outperforms chasing it.

The archive is the key. It is the shared memory of where search has been. Without it, the search is a random walk. With it, the search systematically maps the unknown — drawn toward unexplored regions the way water finds the lowest ground.

Stepping stones don't look like progress. Vacuum tubes don't look like computers. Cowpox doesn't look like a smallpox vaccine. The worm swarming paper doesn't look like a breakthrough in how machines learn. On deceptive landscapes, the gradient is the enemy. Novelty search ignores the gradient and maps the space.

The archive — the shared record of where the field has searched and failed — does not exist in science. Negative results are unpublishable. Cross-disciplinary combinations are unfundable. We are running gradient ascent with no archive, on a landscape we haven't mapped, toward goals that may be in regions the gradient will never point toward.

The novelty score of a candidate solution x is:

ρ(x) = (1/k) Σᵢ dist(x, μᵢ) Where μᵢ are the k nearest neighbours in the archive of prior solutions and dist is behavioural distance — how differently the solutions actually behave, not how different their parameters are. Only candidates above a novelty threshold enter the archive permanently.

Stanley and Lehman's key finding: this strategy not only avoids local maxima — it often finds the global solution faster than direct objective-based search, precisely because it does not commit to a direction prematurely.[8,9]

Applied to science: the archive is the shared failure log. The novelty scorer is what asks "has anyone tried this before?" The exploration bonus is protected time. The behavioural distance is cross-disciplinary difference. Each component already exists in some form. What doesn't exist is the system that connects them.

VI.

The Experiment

Theory without a test is just a preference. Here is what to actually build.

The people. Select 50 early-career researchers across neuroscience, physics, machine learning, materials science, and adjacent fields. Early-career matters: they have not yet calcified around a single paradigm, and the experiment's outcomes are most visible over the multi-decade arc of a career that is just beginning. Randomly assign to two cohorts of 25.

Control cohort: Standard grant expectations — milestones, quarterly reports, specialist evaluation, conventional publication outputs.

Search cohort: Three structural modifications only.

Protected exploration time — 40% of funded hours. No deliverables. No hypothesis required in advance. The only requirement: when something clicks, log the cognitive state that produced it. Not the idea — the state. Were you reading? Walking? Connecting unrelated fields? The brain pays 20% of its energy budget to run its exploration system even at rest. We propose paying 40% of researcher time to protect the equivalent.

Shared failure intelligence. Every experiment — successful or failed — logged in a structured, AI-queryable database. Hypothesis, method, result, what it rules out, what to try next. This is the archive that novelty search requires. My 250 hours of repeated failed experiments become visible. The next researcher finds the log before repeating the same work.

Monthly cross-disciplinary salons with novelty scoring. Each researcher presents their most surprising anomaly — not their best result, but the thing that doesn't fit. An LLM-assisted novelty scorer measures distance from the archive and surfaces the highest-novelty combinations for structured discussion. The salon's job is not to evaluate — it is to collide.

Why this has worked before. Bell Labs (1925–1984) combined deep technical specialisation with deliberate physical proximity across disciplines — mathematicians, physicists, chemists, and engineers sharing corridors and problems. The transistor, information theory, the laser, Unix — all emerged from this collision structure.[10] DARPA has funded high-variance research programs since 1958 with explicit tolerance for failure — program managers given a mission, three to five years, and no publication requirements.[11] Xerox PARC generated the graphical user interface, ethernet, and object-oriented programming — none of which required conventional publication metrics to begin.[12] Each is an existence proof, not a blueprint. But each shares a common structure: elite talent, protected time, cross-disciplinary collision, and tolerance for search that doesn't immediately look like progress.

The feedback loop. Measure over 24 months: how many researchers produce genuinely novel framings that exit their current optimisation game — assessed by independent reviewers blind to cohort assignment. How often does the shared failure database prevent repeated experiments. Whether breakthroughs can be traced back through the exploration logs. Plus standard outputs: papers, tools, collaborations, follow-on grants.

What makes it scale. Three conditions transfer this beyond a pilot: the archive must be a public good — open infrastructure closer in spirit to arXiv than to a proprietary database; novelty scoring must be field-sensitive, a solvable problem given current LLM capabilities; and evaluation must reward process, not just output — if search cohort researchers are judged by the same metrics as the control cohort, the structural modification will be undermined from within.

What could make it fail. Researchers may not adopt the exploration logging format. The failure database may be too heterogeneous for LLM-assisted reasoning. The monthly salons may calcify into performance rather than genuine collision. The 40% exploration time may produce anxiety rather than insight. These are real failure modes. Each one would tell us something important about what the actual solution needs to look like.

That is how science should study science.

If no measurable gains appear after 24 months, learn from it and stop. If gains appear, scale the components that worked independently across fields. Publish everything — including the failures. Run the experiment on the experiment.

VII.

Three Objections Worth Taking Seriously

Most speculative ideas fail. Correct. But exploration should be evaluated as a portfolio, not by the success rate of any single attempt. A venture fund that only backed certain winners would never have funded the internet. The question is not whether individual speculative bets fail — they will — but whether the portfolio generates returns that narrow search cannot.

Incremental work is essential. Absolutely. Replication, refinement, and careful engineering are the foundation of reliable knowledge. The problem is not exploitation. It is monoculture — when an entire field converges on the same hill, the same methods, the same metrics, without anyone assigned to look elsewhere.

Exploration can be gamed. So can publication metrics. So can citation counts. So can grant applications. Every evaluation system has failure modes. The solution is better design, not pretending current systems are neutral or complete.

§

Modern science has built powerful machinery for productivity. It can specialise, refine, benchmark, and optimise with extraordinary competence.

What it has built less deliberately is machinery for exploration.

I found the insight that drives my research in a daydream. I was not being paid. No milestone depended on it.

It was the most productive hour of my last five months.

The next great scientific instrument may not be another microscope, collider, or model.

It may be a better search architecture for discovery itself.
