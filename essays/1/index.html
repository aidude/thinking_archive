I was doing nothing.

Not reading a paper. Not running an experiment. I was letting my mind drift — across worm biology, swarm robotics, warfare, neuromodulation — when something clicked that months of structured work hadn't produced. A connection between fields I had no reason to juxtapose. An insight that arrived not from effort but from its absence.

That daydream became the spine of my research.

This bothered me. It should bother you too.

Because if the most important cognitive event in months of work happened during the one hour nobody was measuring — with no lab, no grant, no advisor, working alone in Bengaluru on rented GPU clusters — then we have a serious structural problem. Not a personal one. A systemic one.

§

I.

The $12,000,000 Problem

In recent conflicts, Iranian drones costing approximately $5,000 each have required THAAD interceptor missiles costing approximately $12,000,000 per shot to neutralise.

Read that again. Five thousand versus twelve million dollars.

A 2,400-to-one cost asymmetry. The defender cannot win this game through optimisation. Better missiles cost more. Cheaper drones cost less. The gradient — the direction of improvement — points confidently upward, and it leads straight into a trap. This is not a failure of engineering. It is a failure of search strategy.

The emerging solution is laser-based directed energy defence. Cost per intercept: less than $1. A 12,000,000× cost reduction — achieved not by improving the existing missile but by returning to physical first principles. Someone stopped climbing the missile hill long enough to ask a different question — and found a completely different mountain.

In physics, this is called a phase transition. The system doesn't improve — it reorganises entirely. Water doesn't become "more liquid" as temperature drops. It becomes ice. A qualitatively different thing, same molecules, completely different game. The mathematics:

The Bifurcation Equation

V(x, c) = x⁴ + c·x²

When c is positive: one obvious minimum — one solution, one hill to climb.
When c crosses zero: the landscape reorganises.
When c is negative: two minima emerge. The second one is the laser.

The constraint — limited budget, physical law, export ban — doesn't slow the search. It changes the shape of the solution space. A second minimum appears that was always mathematically present but invisible to anyone climbing the first hill.

II.

The Same Thing Just Happened in AI

In January 2025, DeepSeek published a model matching GPT-4 on most benchmarks. Training cost: $6 million. GPT-4's estimated cost: $80–100 million. GPU hours used: 2.78 million versus approximately 60 million — a 21× difference.

The reason: the United States government banned export of NVIDIA's most powerful chips to China. DeepSeek couldn't buy the weapon. So they had to think differently.

What constraint forced them to invent

Sparse computation (Mixture of Experts) — instead of running all 671 billion parameters for every calculation, only 37 billion activate at a time. The constraint forced an architecture that dense-model teams had no incentive to pursue.

Lower precision training — 8-bit arithmetic at this scale had never been validated before. Memory limits forced them to try it. It worked. Memory halved, efficiency doubled.

A new communication algorithm — their chips had slower connections than the banned ones. So they invented a method that hides communication delays behind computation. Near-zero overhead on inferior hardware.

None of these came from having more resources. They came from having fewer. The export ban was the constraint wall — the c < 0 in the bifurcation equation. It blocked the obvious path and forced navigation toward a region of design space that well-resourced labs had no incentive to explore.

This is not a story about ingenuity. It is a story about search topology.

III.

Science Is Climbing the Wrong Hill

Bloom et al. (2020) measured something alarming. Research productivity — the number of researchers required to produce a given rate of improvement — has doubled approximately every 13 years since the 1930s across multiple fields. Moore's Law required 18 researchers in 1971 to sustain its pace. By 2014, 18 times more were needed.

We are running faster to stand still. Drag the slider below to see what this looks like across nine decades.

By 2020: ~18× more researchers needed to sustain the same rate of progress as 1930.

This is what a system approaching a local maximum looks like from the inside. The machinery gets bigger. The marginal output gets smaller. Nobody questions the hill because the hill is measurable and being climbed.

Here is how the trap forms. A standard research grant requires a specific hypothesis stated in advance, a methodology committed to before results exist, milestones at fixed intervals, and publications as evidence of productivity. Every one of these requirements is architecturally optimised for exploitation — squeezing value from known territory — and hostile to exploration — finding territory nobody has mapped.

The failure modes are specific. I have experienced them working on brain-inspired AI:

Negative results evaporate. I have spent approximately 250 hours repeating experiments that others had almost certainly already abandoned — silently, invisibly, because the field has no infrastructure for sharing structured failure. The next researcher will repeat the same failures because they are unpublishable.

Interdisciplinary work falls between categories. Research spanning computational neuroscience, statistical physics, and machine learning fits no single grant mechanism. No single review panel covers all three. The most interesting work falls between the cracks of every existing funding structure.

The daydream is treated as waste. The connection that became my research spine arrived during unstructured time. No grant funded it. No milestone captured it. It is structurally invisible to every metric science uses to measure progress.

This is not a funding problem. It is a search strategy problem.

IV.

The Brain Already Solved This

The brain is the only known system that has solved continuous learning, world modelling, and generalisation simultaneously — on 20 watts, across a human lifetime.

It does this through architecturally separated search modes, coordinated by a chemical switching system.

The exploitation system — prefrontal cortex, focused attention, high acetylcholine — is precise and fast. This is you debugging code, running a planned experiment, writing a grant application. It climbs known hills efficiently.

The exploration system — called the Default Mode Network — is stranger. Raichle et al. (2001) discovered it when they noticed certain brain regions become more active when subjects are told to do nothing. It consumes approximately 20% of total brain energy at rest. The brain pays a significant metabolic price to keep this system running when you're staring at the ceiling.

Why? Because it performs a function the exploitation system cannot: connecting things that have no obvious connection. It holds everything you've ever learned and looks for non-obvious patterns across the whole. It is how worm biology connects to swarm robotics connects to how artificial neural networks forget.

Jung-Beeman et al. (2004) captured the moment of insight in real time. The "aha moment" is preceded by the brain actively suppressing its focused attention system — quieting the exploitation machinery so the exploration system can make the connection.

A neuromodulatory system — dopamine, acetylcholine, norepinephrine, serotonin — acts as the meta-controller, switching between the two modes based on what the moment requires. Acetylcholine high: focus and exploit. Default mode active: wander and explore.

We are studying this system. We are trying to replicate it in machines. We are doing this research inside institutions that eliminated the exploration mode entirely — and then wonder why the breakthroughs have slowed.

V.

The Algorithm Science Is Missing

Kenneth Stanley and Joel Lehman spent years building robot controllers using evolutionary algorithms. They kept hitting the same wall: the more precisely they defined the goal, the worse the search performed on complex problems. The algorithm climbed confidently toward the objective — and got stuck.

So they tried something radical. They removed the objective entirely.

Instead of rewarding progress toward a goal, they rewarded only one thing: being different from everything that had come before. They called this novelty search. Each candidate solution is scored by its average distance from an ever-growing archive of everything previously explored. The most novel candidate — not the best, not the closest to the goal — wins.

How Novelty Is Scored

ρ(x) = (1/k) Σᵢ dist(x, μᵢ)

ρ(x) is the novelty score of candidate x. μᵢ are its k nearest neighbours in the archive of prior solutions. dist is how different the behaviours are. Only candidates above a novelty threshold enter the archive permanently. The search is rewarded purely for unexplored territory — never for proximity to the goal.

The result was counterintuitive and reproducible: on deceptive landscapes, ignoring the objective outperforms chasing it. The archive — the shared memory of where search has been — turns out to be more valuable than the gradient toward the goal.

Why does this work? Because complex landscapes are deceptive. The most direct path toward the goal often leads away from it. Stepping stones don't look like progress. Vacuum tubes don't look like computers. Cowpox doesn't look like a smallpox vaccine. The worm swarming paper doesn't look like a continual learning breakthrough.

Current scientific infrastructure is gradient ascent on a deceptive landscape. Grant committees reward proximity to stated objectives. Reviewers reward advancement of declared agendas. The archive — the record of where the field has searched and failed — doesn't exist. Negative results are unpublishable. Cross-disciplinary combinations are unfundable.

We are running gradient ascent with no archive, on a landscape we haven't mapped, toward goals that are almost certainly in regions the gradient will never point toward.

VI.

The Experiment

Theory is cheap. Here is what to actually do.

Select 50 early-career researchers across neuroscience, physics, machine learning, and adjacent fields. Randomly assign to two cohorts.

Control cohort: Standard grant expectations — milestones, quarterly reports, conventional publication outputs, specialist evaluation.

Search cohort: Three structural modifications only.

40% protected exploration time. No deliverables. No hypothesis required in advance. The only requirement: when something clicks — when the daydream produces a connection — log the cognitive state that produced it. Not the idea. The state. Were you reading? Walking? Connecting unrelated fields? The brain pays 20% of its energy budget to run its exploration system even at rest. We propose paying 40% of researcher time to protect the equivalent.

Shared failure intelligence. Every experiment — successful or failed — logged in a structured, AI-queryable database. What was tried, what failed, what it rules out, what to try next. My 250 hours of repeated failed experiments become visible. The next researcher finds the log before repeating the same work.

Monthly cross-disciplinary salons with novelty scoring. Each researcher presents their most surprising anomaly — not their best result, but the thing that doesn't fit. An LLM-assisted novelty scorer measures distance from the archive and surfaces the highest-novelty combinations for structured discussion.

Measure over 24 months: how many researchers produce genuinely novel framings that exit their current optimisation game — assessed by independent reviewers blind to cohort assignment. How often failure logs prevent repeated experiments. Whether breakthroughs trace back through the daydream logs. Plus standard metrics: papers, patents, collaborations, follow-on funding.

The experiment can fail. Researchers may not adopt the format. The failure database may be too heterogeneous. The novelty scorer may surface useless combinations. These are real failure modes — and each one tells us something important about what the actual solution needs to look like.

That is how science should study science.

If no gains appear after 24 months, learn and stop. If gains appear, scale. Publish everything — the failures included. Run the experiment on the experiment.

§

We often imagine scientific progress as a straight line powered by more money, more talent, more compute. Add inputs, get outputs.

But history suggests a different engine: intelligent constraints, asymmetric search, and freedom to explore strange terrain.

The brain figured this out 500 million years ago. It built two systems — exploitation and exploration — and a chemical switching mechanism between them. It pays 20% of its energy budget to keep the exploration system running even at rest. It protects the daydream because the daydream is doing real work.

Modern science has built powerful machinery for productivity. It has built almost no machinery for exploration.

I found the insight that drives my research in a daydream. I was not being paid. No milestone depended on it.

It was the most productive hour of my last five months.

We should stop building better THAAD missiles.

The next breakthrough may not require more force. It may require escaping the hill we already know how to climb.
